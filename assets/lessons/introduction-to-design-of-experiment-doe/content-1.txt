<section id="foundations">
      <div class="section-head">
        <div>
          <div class="section-kicker">Part 1 · Foundations</div>
          <h2>What DOE is designed to reveal</h2>
          <p class="lead">
            Design of Experiments changes several controllable inputs in a planned pattern so that their
            individual and combined effects on a response can be estimated efficiently.
          </p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="grid-3">
        <article class="card concept-card" data-number="1">
          <h3>Factor</h3>
          <p>A controllable input such as time, force, current, temperature, speed, or chemistry.</p>
          <div class="pill-row">
            <span class="pill">A: Time</span>
            <span class="pill">B: Force</span>
            <span class="pill">C: Current</span>
          </div>
        </article>

        <article class="card concept-card" data-number="2">
          <h3>Level</h3>
          <p>A setting of a factor. In a two-level design, each factor has a low and high setting.</p>
          <div class="equation">Low = −1 &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; High = +1</div>
        </article>

        <article class="card concept-card" data-number="3">
          <h3>Response</h3>
          <p>The measured outcome, such as strength, yield, deviation, cycle time, or defect rate.</p>
          <div class="pill-row">
            <span class="pill">Y: Strength</span>
            <span class="pill">Goal: maximize</span>
          </div>
        </article>
      </div>

      <div class="grid-2 mt-20">
        <div class="card card-pad">
          <h3>Why factorial experimentation?</h3>
          <p>
            A factorial design tests every combination of the selected factor levels. This makes it possible
            to estimate interactions—the cases where the effect of one factor changes depending on another factor.
          </p>
          <div class="equation">
            Number of treatment combinations = 2<sup>k</sup>
          </div>
          <p class="small muted mb-0">For two factors: 2² = 4 combinations. For three factors: 2³ = 8 combinations.</p>
        </div>

        <div class="card card-pad">
          <h3>Interaction: the central idea</h3>
          <p>
            An interaction exists when the effect of factor A is not constant across the levels of factor B.
            On an interaction plot, nonparallel lines indicate interaction.
          </p>
          <div class="equation">
            AB effect = average response when AB = +1 − average response when AB = −1
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="notice mt-20">
        <strong>Terminology caution:</strong> a treatment combination is a unique factor-level combination.
        A run is the physical execution of that combination. Replicating a combination creates additional runs,
        but not additional treatment combinations.
      </div>
    </section>
