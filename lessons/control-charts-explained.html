Control Charts, Explained

What you will learn: how to tell the difference between normal process variation and a real problem.

Lean Six Sigma

By the end, you should be able to

  • Read a basic control chart.
  • Explain the difference between normal variation and a real shift.
  • Decide when a data point deserves investigation.

Why this matters at work

Every process has some natural up-and-down. Reacting to every wiggle wastes time; ignoring a real shift lets defects slip through. Control charts give you a clear line between "normal" and "something changed."

Simple explanation

Plot your data over time against a center line and control limits.

Center line: the process average.
Control limits: roughly ±3 standard deviations from the average.
Points inside the limits, moving randomly, are normal variation. A point outside the limits — or a clear trend — signals a real change worth investigating.

Real workplace example

A call center's handle time jumps

Average handle time normally bounces between 4 and 6 minutes every day.

  1. Normal day: handle time sits between 4–6 minutes — inside the control limits, no action needed.
  2. Signal: handle time jumps to 9 minutes for three days straight, above the upper control limit.
  3. Investigate: something changed — new system, new staff, new process — and it deserves a look.

Common mistake

Watch out for: reacting to every single up-and-down point as if it were a real problem. That's normal variation — chasing it wastes time and can make the process worse.

Try it yourself

Take 15–20 recent data points for something you track (handle time, defect count, response time). Plot them on a simple line chart with the average marked. Do any points look like outliers, or is it all normal noise?

Check your understanding

A single data point falls just slightly below yesterday's value, still well within the control limits. What should you do?
