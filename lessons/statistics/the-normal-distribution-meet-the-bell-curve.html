The Normal Distribution: Meet the Bell Curve

StatisticsBeginner15 minutesInteractive

The normal distribution — the famous “bell curve” — describes how many real-world measurements naturally spread around an average. In this lesson you’ll learn to read the curve, use the 68–95–99.7 rule, and turn any measurement into a z-score you can interpret in seconds.

Why this matters: heights, exam marks, measurement errors, filling volumes, cycle times — a huge amount of workplace data is roughly bell-shaped. Once you can read that shape, you can answer questions like “how unusual is this result?” without ever opening a statistics textbook.

Learning objectives

By the end of this lesson you will be able to:

  1. Identify the key features of a normal distribution (symmetry, one peak, mean = median = mode).
  2. Explain what the mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ) control in a bell curve.
  3. Apply the 68–95–99.7 rule to estimate what proportion of data falls in a given range.
  4. Calculate a z-score for any measurement and interpret what it means.
  5. Select situations where the normal model is (and is not) a sensible assumption.

Prerequisites

You should be comfortable with the idea of an average (mean) and basic arithmetic (subtraction and division). That’s it. If you have met the standard deviation before, great — but we recap it below, so no prior statistics knowledge is required.

The idea in plain English

Measure the height of 1,000 adults and draw a bar chart of the results. You won’t get a random mess — you’ll get a very particular shape: most people are close to the average, and the further you move from the average, the fewer people you find. The result is a smooth, symmetric hill: the bell curve.

What it is: the normal (or Gaussian) distribution is a mathematical model for data that clusters symmetrically around an average. It is completely described by just two numbers: the mean and the standard deviation.

Why it is used: it appears when a result is influenced by many small, independent factors. Examples include heights, measurement errors, stable fill volumes, and many test scores.

What question it answers: “How common or unusual is a particular value?”

Where it is used: quality control, specification analysis, forecasting, measurement systems, and statistical testing.

What it does not tell us: the normal curve is a model, not a law. Skewed, bounded, or multi-peaked data may require a different model.

The formula (gently)

The height of the bell curve at any value x is:

f(x) =1σ√(2π)· e−(x − μ)² / 2σ²

  • f(x) curve density at x
  • μ mean
  • σ standard deviation, greater than 0

Plain-language interpretation: values close to the mean have greater density; values far from the mean become increasingly rare.

The z-score formula

z =x − μσ

The z-score has no units. Its sign shows direction and its magnitude shows distance from the mean.

The 68–95–99.7 rule

  • About 68% of values fall within ±1σ.
  • About 95% fall within ±2σ.
  • About 99.7% fall within ±3σ.

Step-by-step: how unusual is this value?

  1. Check the shape. Look for one peak and rough symmetry.
  2. Find μ and σ.
  3. Select the method. Use the empirical rule for whole standard deviations; otherwise calculate z.
  4. Calculate z = (x − μ) / σ.
  5. Convert to a proportion. Use the rule, a z-table, or software.
  6. Interpret in context.
  7. Document the source, mean, standard deviation, and sample size.

Worked example: is this exam score exceptional?

Problem: Priya scored 88 on an exam with mean 72 and standard deviation 8.

z =88 − 728= 2.0

The score is 2 standard deviations above the mean. By the empirical rule, about 2.5% of scores exceed +2σ, so Priya scored above approximately 97.5% of candidates.

Limitation: the conclusion assumes historical scores are approximately normal and still representative.

Interpretation guide

When you seeIt means
z = 0The value is exactly average.
z between −1 and +1Ordinary; about 68% of normal values are here.
|z| beyond 2Unusual and worth a closer look.
|z| beyond 3Rare; investigate the process or measurement.
One peak and symmetryThe normal model is plausible.
Strong skew or two peaksThe normal model may be inappropriate.

Common mistakes and exam traps

Assuming everything is normal Assumption error

Plot the data before using normal probabilities.

Using variance instead of standard deviation Calculation error

Divide by σ, not σ².

Confusing below with above Interpretation error

Software normally returns cumulative probability below x; subtract from 1 for the upper tail.

Dropping the minus sign Calculation error

The sign identifies which side of the mean contains the value.

Treating 68–95–99.7 as exact for any data Exam trap

It is an approximation for normal data only.

Using density instead of cumulative probability Software error

In Excel, use TRUE for cumulative probability.

Software guidance: Excel and Minitab

Practice activity: the bottling line

A filling process has μ = 350 ml and σ = 10 ml and is approximately normal.

  1. What percentage lies between 340 and 360 ml?
  2. What percentage exceeds 370 ml?
  3. What percentage is below 320 ml?

1. About 68%. The limits are μ ± 1σ.

2. About 2.5%. 370 ml is μ + 2σ; one upper tail contains half of the 5% outside ±2σ.

3. About 0.15%. 320 ml is μ − 3σ; one lower tail contains half of the 0.3% outside ±3σ.

Final summary

  • The normal distribution is symmetric and single-peaked.
  • μ controls the centre; σ controls the spread.
  • Use 68–95–99.7 for whole standard-deviation bands.
  • Use z = (x − μ) / σ for any value.
  • |z| beyond 2 is unusual; beyond 3 is rare.
  • Plot the data before assuming normality.
Remember this: subtract the mean, divide by σ — then let 68–95–99.7 do the talking.
