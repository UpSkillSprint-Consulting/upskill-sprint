The Normal Distribution: Meet the Bell Curve

StatisticsBeginner15 minutesInteractive

The normal distribution — the famous “bell curve” — describes how many real-world measurements naturally spread around an average. In this lesson you’ll learn to read the curve, use the 68–95–99.7 rule, and turn any measurement into a z-score you can interpret in seconds.

Why this matters: heights, exam marks, measurement errors, filling volumes, cycle times — a huge amount of workplace data is roughly bell-shaped. Once you can read that shape, you can answer questions like “how unusual is this result?” without ever opening a statistics textbook.

Learning objectives

By the end of this lesson you will be able to:

  1. Identify the key features of a normal distribution.
  2. Explain what the mean and standard deviation control.
  3. Apply the 68–95–99.7 rule.
  4. Calculate and interpret a z-score.
  5. Select when the normal model is sensible.

Prerequisites

No prior statistics knowledge is required beyond the idea of an average and basic arithmetic.

The idea in plain English

Measure the height of 1,000 adults and most values will cluster near the average, with fewer values farther away. The result is a smooth, symmetric hill: the bell curve.

What it is: a mathematical model for data that clusters symmetrically around an average.

Why it is used: many outcomes combine numerous small independent influences.

What it answers: how common or unusual a value is.

What it does not tell us: not all data is normal; skewed, bounded, or mixed data needs different tools.

Key terminology

TermPlain-language meaningPractical interpretation
Mean (μ)The average.The centre of the curve.
Standard deviation (σ)A typical distance from the average.Small σ is narrow; large σ is wide.
z-scoreStandard deviations from the mean.Positive is above; negative is below.
Empirical ruleThe 68–95–99.7 shortcut.Estimates proportions for normal data.

The formula (gently)

z =x − μσ

  • z distance from the mean in standard deviations
  • x the value examined
  • μ mean
  • σ standard deviation, greater than zero

Subtract the mean, then divide by the typical spread.

The 68–95–99.7 rule

  • About 68% lie within ±1σ.
  • About 95% lie within ±2σ.
  • About 99.7% lie within ±3σ.

Example: for mean 170 cm and standard deviation 8 cm, about 68% fall between 162 and 178 cm.

Step-by-step procedure

  1. Check the histogram for one peak and rough symmetry.
  2. Find the mean and standard deviation.
  3. Use the empirical rule at whole standard-deviation boundaries.
  4. Otherwise calculate z = (x − μ) / σ.
  5. Convert the z-score to a percentile.
  6. Interpret the result in context.

Worked example: is this exam score exceptional?

Priya scored 88 on an exam with mean 72 and standard deviation 8.

z =88 − 728= 2

Her score is 2 standard deviations above the mean, roughly the 97.5th percentile using the empirical rule.

Interpretation guide

When you seeIt means
z = 0Exactly average.
|z| < 1Ordinary.
|z| > 2Unusual.
|z| > 3Rare.

Common mistakes

Assuming everything is normal

Always inspect the shape first.

Using variance instead of standard deviation

Check units before standardising.

Confusing below with above

Software usually returns cumulative area below.

Dropping the negative sign

The sign identifies which side of the mean.

Treating the empirical rule as exact

It is an approximation for normal data.

Software guidance

In Excel use =STANDARDIZE(x, mean, sd) for z and =NORM.DIST(x, mean, sd, TRUE) for the proportion below x.

Practice activity

A bottling machine has mean 350 ml and standard deviation 10 ml. Estimate the percentage between 340 and 360 ml, above 370 ml, and below 320 ml.

Between 340 and 360: about 68%. Above 370: about 2.5%. Below 320: about 0.15%.

Final summary

  • The curve is symmetric and centred on the mean.
  • The standard deviation controls spread.
  • Remember 68–95–99.7.
  • Use z = (x − μ) / σ.
  • Check normality before using the model.
