The 7 Essential Quality Tools

Learn the tools as a connected problem-solving system—not as isolated definitions. Diagnose a steel tensile-failure problem, manipulate live charts, choose the next tool, and practice the loop-back decisions that separate strong investigations from weak ones.

The core sequence

Each tool answers one question. The answer determines the next move.

1 FlowchartWhere can it originate?
2 Check SheetHow will we collect evidence?
3 StratificationWhich subgroup carries the issue?
4 ParetoWhat is the vital few?
5 FishboneWhat could cause it?
6 HistogramWhat is the distribution doing?
7 Scatter PlotDo X and Y move together?
8 Control ChartDid the fix hold?
7 vs. 8: references differ on whether stratification, flowchart, or run chart occupies the seventh slot. In field work, use all eight capabilities. Stratification is non-negotiable.

Learning objectives

  • Select a tool from the question being asked.
  • Build a defensible tool-to-tool sequence.
  • Recognize what each tool cannot prove.
  • Loop back when evidence rejects a hypothesis.

Interactive tool selector

Histogram laboratory

Shift the mean, widen the spread, and inject outliers. Watch how the shape changes before deciding what the pattern means.

Interpretation rule

A histogram shows shape, spread, and centering. It discards time order and does not prove cause.

Bimodal? Do not call it “high variation” yet. First ask whether two populations were mixed.

Pareto example

Shift C failure modes are ranked by frequency.

Scatter-plot laboratory

Increase or reduce the apparent relationship. Then decide whether the evidence is strong enough to justify an engineering trial.

Critical caution: correlation supports a hypothesis; it does not establish causation. Check physics, subgrouping, range, confounders, and measurement quality.

Steel investigation challenge

A final tensile test detects low yield strength. Your job is to decide what happens next.

  1. Map EAF → caster → reheat → rolling → cooling → sizing → tensile test.
  2. Collect heat, grade, shift, finish temperature, cooling zone, orientation, date/time, and failure mode.
  3. Stratify before building global charts.
  4. Build the fishbone around the Pareto-confirmed problem—not “tensile failures” broadly.
  5. Confirm with data, then verify stability.

Knowledge check

A fishbone branch says “low Mn causes low YS.” What is the correct next action?
