Root Cause Analysis: 5 Whys vs Fishbone Diagram

What you will learn: two simple ways to trace a problem back to its real cause, and when to use each.

Quality Engineering

By the end, you should be able to

  • Run a 5 Whys on a recurring problem.
  • Know when a Fishbone diagram fits better than 5 Whys.
  • Avoid stopping at the first easy answer.

Why this matters at work

Fixing symptoms instead of causes means the same problem keeps coming back. Root cause analysis stops the cycle by forcing you to ask "why" until you hit something you can actually act on.

Simple explanation

Both tools get you from symptom to cause — they just work differently.

5 Whys: ask "why did that happen?" five times in a row, each answer feeding the next question.
Fishbone (Ishikawa): branches the same question into categories — people, process, equipment, materials — so you can spot causes across multiple angles at once.

Real workplace example

A machine keeps jamming

Tracing the real cause with 5 Whys:

  1. Why does it jam? Debris builds up inside.
  2. Why does debris build up? The filter isn't cleaned.
  3. Why isn't it cleaned? No one owns that task — it was never assigned after a staffing change.

The real fix is assigning ownership, not just cleaning the machine once.

Common mistake

Watch out for: stopping at the first "why" that sounds reasonable. The first answer is usually a symptom, not the root cause — keep going until the answer points to something you can actually change.

Try it yourself

Pick a recurring annoyance at work. Ask "why" five times, writing down each answer. See how far the real cause is from your first guess.

Check your understanding

You need to compare causes across people, process, equipment, and materials at once. Which tool fits best?
