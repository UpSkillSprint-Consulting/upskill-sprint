How to Use the Steel Phase & Transformation Explorer

Move from chemistry and processing conditions to predicted microstructure, properties, and performance. The explorer combines equilibrium and kinetic transformation analysis, applied heat-treatment models, thermal-data analysis, measurement guidance, metallurgy laboratories, a source-faithful rapid-quench reconstruction, and the complete Buehler iron-carbon poster with interactive overlays.

Before you begin

Purpose and limits

The explorer is a teaching and preliminary engineering-screening environment organized around:

Chemistry → Processing → Microstructure → Properties → Performance

Best uses

Learning metallurgy, comparing scenarios, phase-field identification, heat-treatment visualization, process-data screening, measurement troubleshooting, and early-stage hypothesis development.

Not a replacement for

Controlled standards, certified grade data, validated thermodynamic or kinetic software, calibrated measurements, laboratory testing, procedure qualification, material disposition, or responsible engineering approval.

Engineering caution: calculations and interactive overlays are educational models. Never use the tool alone to release material, qualify a process, set hazardous operating conditions, or approve a product.

Interpretation basis

Use Equilibrium for Fe–Fe3C phase fields and lever-rule fractions. Use Rapid cooling for generalized ferrite, pearlite, bainite, martensite, and retained-austenite tendencies.

Critical-temperature terminology

  • Ae: equilibrium critical temperature.
  • Ac: observed transformation during heating.
  • Ar: observed transformation during cooling.
  • Ms/Mf: martensite start and finish concepts.

Core modules

Equilibrium, heat-treatment paths, TTT/CCT, and chemistry

Equilibrium diagram

  1. Select metric or imperial display.
  2. Enter carbon and temperature.
  3. Use + Add point for comparisons.
  4. Select a row or marker to make a point active.
  5. Click or drag the diagram to reposition it.

Use tie lines, spotlighting, critical-boundary snapping, connected paths, lever-rule fractions, and the schematic microstructure viewer.

fα = (Cγ − C₀)/(Cγ − Cα)    and    fγ = (C₀ − Cα)/(Cγ − Cα)

Heating and cooling paths

Select a preset or build a custom sequence of heating, holding, cooling, quenching, and tempering steps. Use Play, Pause, or the time slider, then review boundary crossings and the live schematic.

TTT and CCT

TTT

Use isothermal temperature and hold time to examine idealized transformation start and finish behaviour.

CCT

Use cooling rate and final temperature to compare ferrite, pearlite, bainite, martensite, and retained-austenite tendencies.

Chemistry and causal chain

Enter verified chemistry to screen CE IIW, Pcm, Ac1, Ac3, Ms, hardenability, weldability, and directional properties. The causal dashboard links chemistry, processing, microstructure, properties, and performance.

Release 2

Applied heat-treatment modules

Jominy and section-size hardenability

Select geometry, section size, quench medium, agitation, prior-austenite grain number, and target hardness. Read the estimated Jominy curve, equivalent Jominy distance, martensite fraction, and surface-to-centre hardness profile.

Austenitization and grain-growth window

Set temperature, hold time, section size, heating rate, starting microstructure, carbide burden, pinning, and initial grain size. Balance heat-through, dissolution, homogenization, and grain growth.

Quench-medium comparison

Compare air/gas, oils, polymer, water, brine, and the salt-bath analogue under common assumptions. Review surface, quarter-depth, and centre cooling, martensite, hardness, thermal gradients, cracking risk, and distortion risk.

Safety: the tool does not provide hazardous quench, hot-oil, brine, polymer, molten-salt, or high-temperature operating instructions.

Release 3

Process-data analysis and temperature measurement

Thermal-data upload

  1. Open Process Data.
  2. Drop or select a CSV, TSV, TXT, or semicolon-delimited file.
  3. Map time and temperature columns.
  4. Select source units.
  5. Adjust smoothing and arrest sensitivity.
Cooling rate = −dT/dt

Review cooling-rate windows, candidate arrests, proximity to critical temperatures, measured-versus-simulated paths, and RMSE. Candidate arrests are screening signals—not confirmed transformations.

Measurement assistant

Use thermocouple mode for type, diameter, attachment, shielding, thermal mass, and response-time risks. Use pyrometer mode for emissivity, reflected temperature, target-to-spot ratio, and surface-condition errors.

Metallurgy laboratories

Surface and diffusion

Model carburizing, carbonitriding, nitriding, ferritic nitrocarburizing, induction, flame, and laser hardening trends.

Tempering

Explore hardness loss, toughness recovery, stress relief, retained austenite, secondary hardening, and embrittlement.

Mechanical tests

Use stress–strain, Charpy/DBTT, fatigue S–N, and hardness-conversion learning views.

Virtual metallography

Generate schematic ferrite, pearlite, bainite, martensite, spheroidite, decarburization, banding, and inclusions.

Solidification

Animate dendrites, segregation, porosity, rolling elongation, banding, and anisotropy.

Alloy families

Use family-specific indicators for plain/low-alloy, stainless, tool steel, and cast iron.

Release 5

Source-faithful interactive reference diagrams

The Interactive Diagrams tab prioritizes visual fidelity to the supplied references rather than presenting a simplified substitute.

Rapid-quench reconstruction

The chart is reconstructed in its native 850 × 890 portrait coordinate system. The title, graph rectangle, dual °C/°F axes, grid density, carbon scale, color fields, region geometry, critical curves, callouts, and label placement follow the supplied figure.

Buehler iron-carbon poster

The complete Buehler poster is displayed in its original portrait proportions with its title, phase fields, heat-color column, crystal structures, micrographs, axes, logos, and source information visually intact. The explorer adds non-destructive interactive overlays above it.

Publication-rights caution: source ownership and reproduction permission remain separate from technical implementation. Confirm the right to publish the supplied rapid chart and the Buehler poster on a public website before merging or releasing this feature.

The rapid-quench chart is a carbon–temperature teaching map, not a time-resolved TTT or CCT diagram. Interactive classifications and overlays must be verified against validated, grade-specific data.

Accuracy and rights controls

Engineering and publication cautions

  • Separate equilibrium phases from kinetic transformation products.
  • Bainite and martensite are not equilibrium fields on the Fe–Fe3C diagram.
  • Pearlite and bainite are microconstituents, not single phases.
  • Alloying, grain size, prior condition, section size, and cooling history shift real behaviour.
  • Mechanical properties depend on morphology, texture, inclusions, precipitation, sampling, and test method.
  • Candidate thermal arrests and measurement corrections require independent verification.
  • Do not derive hazardous process settings from this educational tool.
  • Confirm public-display and reproduction rights for supplied and third-party reference artwork before production release.

Before relying on an output

Final checklist

  • Confirm carbon and alloy chemistry.
  • Confirm temperature units.
  • Select the correct diagram and interpretation basis.
  • Separate equilibrium phases from kinetic products.
  • Document thermal path, time, and section size.
  • Verify grade-specific TTT/CCT or thermodynamic data.
  • Treat interactive region classifications as educational estimates.
  • Confirm the source image is loaded and the point lies inside the calibrated graph rectangle.
  • Confirm publication rights before public release.
  • Verify with metallography, hardness, chemistry, or mechanical testing.
  • Obtain technical approval where required.
